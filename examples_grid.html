Clothes of the Old Russian princess

A shirt is a garment of a straight cut. The shirts for noble people were made of silk or thin fabric.

Epancha is a long mantle without sleeves. The princely epancha was decorated with fur, precious stones, gold and silver.

A coif is an ancient female headdress that covered the whole head. Sometimes the coif had ties under the chin.

Kokoshnick is the headpiece of married women. Kokoshniks were embroidered or decorated with precious stones, pearls.

A belt is a strip of cloth or leather. The belts of noble people were richly decorated.

Boots. Old Russian boots were often with an upwardly blunt or pointed nose. Boots were decorated with pearls, gold thread. Most often, boots were worn with linen or woolen stockings.

Phrasal verbs

Set off — to begin a journey.

The princess set off on a long journey.

Set sail — to begin a trip on a ship or boat.

They set sail on Aug. 3, 1492 from Spain.

Set foot — to enter, to step on.

Europeans set foot on North America many centuries ago.

Set up — to put together or arrange the parts of something before using it.

It took almost an hour to set up the tent in the rain.

Set to work — to begin doing something.

We have fixed the roof; now we will set to work on the kitchen.

The decomposition of nitrates

Nitrates are decomposed when heated. The composition of the decomposition products depends on the activity of the metal, the cation of which is part of the nitrate, i.e., from its position in the series of standard electrode potentials (the electrochemical series):
